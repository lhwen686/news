利用增强现实激励假肢训练

Using augmented reality to motivate prosthesis training

本研究深入探讨了增强现实（AR）技术在提高假肢训练依从性和功能效率方面的潜力，尤其针对上肢假肢使用者。核心医学机制在于，传统的假肢训练往往缺乏足够的患者动机与个性化指导，导致训练效果不佳及假肢排斥率高。通过引入如微软HoloLens2等AR设备，结合定制化的训练游戏（如ARm-Strong），患者能在模拟环境中进行功能性任务练习，同时保持对现实环境的感知，这不仅降低了安全风险，更显著提升了训练过程的趣味性、专注度和沉浸感。从专业角度看，AR训练通过视觉和运动反馈，强化了患者的本体感觉和运动规划能力，有助于大脑皮层对假肢操作的学习和适应，从而促进神经可塑性。研究结果表明，AR干预组在完成功能任务方面的效率显著高于对照组，这预示着AR能有效改善假肢的功能整合度。其行业影响深远，有望彻底改变当前的假肢康复模式，从被动枯燥的训练转向主动、沉浸式的个性化康复体验。随着AR头显成本的下降，该技术有望在临床康复机构和居家环境中广泛应用，降低医疗成本，提升患者生活质量，并减少昂贵假肢的废弃率，推动物理医学与康复学领域的技术创新和普及。

Artificial limbs look and function more like real limbs than ever before—but that's only helpful if they are used as intended. One of the main reasons amputees give for not using their body-powered prosthesis is a lack of motivation or knowledge of how to properly use them. Part of the reason for this is that compared to the time and resources devoted to improving the comfort and function of prostheses, much less attention is spent on making prosthetic training more effective.

Testing augmented reality for prosthetic training

But Susan D'Andrea, an associate professor of kinesiology at the University of Rhode Island College of Health Sciences, recently undertook a study aimed at trying to rectify this. D'Andrea conducted a feasibility study using an augmented reality game to keep people engaged and motivated during training or rehabilitation.

An article about the study, which the professor co-authored with former URI students Lauren Deus and Leah Wohlbach, was published in the February issue of the journal PLOS One.

"The quality and quantity of prosthetic training have a significant influence on prosthetic acceptance," said D'Andrea. "However, there's no consensus on the most effective delivery for this training."

D'Andrea recruited 32 healthy, able-bodied participants for the study, ranging in age from 18 to 65. Wearing a bypass body-powered prosthetic device, participants performed functional task assessments in D'Andrea's MotionVR Biomechanics Lab.

"We focused on the upper extremities because individuals with upper extremity amputations will often reject their prosthetic," said D'Andrea. "Many times, it feels easier for them to use their uninjured arm because they don't get enough training on using the prosthetic."

Half the participants in the study were randomly assigned to an augmented reality intervention group and the other half to a control group.

Participants in the augmented reality group used the ARm-Strong training game, which was developed at URI by kinesiology and biomedical engineering students. The game challenges participants to stack holographic cups while wearing a Microsoft HoloLens2 headset. They were tested during three visits, with 4–6 days between each visit.

Participants in the control group received no training between pre- and post-measurements of hand function and were instructed not to use a prosthesis between visits to the lab. During their two visits, which were 8–12 days apart, they were instructed to complete a seven-task functional assessment using the prosthesis only. One task was to turn over five index cards on a table while being timed.

"On average, individuals in the augmented reality group were significantly more efficient at using the bypass prosthesis to complete functional tasks compared to the control group," said D'Andrea. "Individuals who engaged in the augmented reality training had positive feelings of engagement, engrossment, and immersion towards the application."

Future potential and accessibility

D'Andrea is hopeful that augmented reality will become widely adopted in health care as a training or rehabilitation tool.

"The amount of time needed to progress through prosthetic training protocol varies from person to person depending on their needs and function. That's why the augmented reality game is appealing—it can be tailored to the needs and abilities of the user," said D'Andrea. "It's also a great solution because you can play the game anywhere."

She also noted that there are very few safety issues because users can see their "real" environment through the lenses, as opposed to virtual reality headsets, where users can only see the game.

While widespread implementation of the technology has been cost-prohibitive to this point, D'Andrea expressed optimism that the technology may soon be available more widely.

"I think the price point of the augmented reality headsets is finally starting to come down, which might speed up the adoption of the technology for rehabilitation," she said.

测试增强现实技术用于假肢训练

但罗德岛大学健康科学学院运动机能学副教授Susan D'Andrea最近进行了一项旨在纠正这一问题的研究。D'Andrea利用增强现实游戏进行了一项可行性研究，以在训练或康复期间保持人们的参与度和积极性。

这项研究的相关文章由该教授与URI前学生Lauren Deus和Leah Wohlbach合著，发表在《PLOS One》杂志的二月刊上。

“假肢训练的质量和数量对假肢的接受度有显著影响，”D'Andrea说。“然而，对于这种训练最有效的实施方式，目前尚未达成共识。”

D'Andrea招募了32名年龄在18至65岁之间的健康、健全的参与者进行这项研究。参与者佩戴旁路体动假肢装置，在D'Andrea的MotionVR生物力学实验室进行了功能任务评估。

“我们专注于上肢，因为上肢截肢者经常会排斥他们的假肢，”D'Andrea说。“很多时候，他们觉得使用未受伤的手臂更容易，因为他们没有获得足够的假肢使用训练。”

研究中一半的参与者被随机分配到增强现实干预组，另一半分配到对照组。

增强现实组的参与者使用了由URI运动机能学和生物医学工程学生开发的ARm-Strong训练游戏。该游戏挑战参与者在佩戴微软HoloLens2头显的情况下堆叠全息杯。他们进行了三次测试，每次访问间隔4-6天。

对照组的参与者在手功能的前后测量之间没有接受任何训练，并被指示在两次实验室访问之间不要使用假肢。在他们两次间隔8-12天的访问中，他们被指示仅使用假肢完成一项七项功能评估。其中一项任务是在计时的情况下翻转桌上的五张索引卡。

“平均而言，与对照组相比，增强现实组的个体在使用旁路假肢完成功能任务方面效率显著更高，”D'Andrea说。“参与增强现实训练的个体对该应用产生了积极的参与感、专注度和沉浸感。”

未来潜力和可及性

D'Andrea希望增强现实技术能作为一种训练或康复工具在医疗保健领域得到广泛应用。

“完成假肢训练方案所需的时间因人而异，取决于他们的需求和功能。这就是增强现实游戏吸引人的地方——它可以根据用户的需求和能力进行调整，”D'Andrea说。“这也是一个很好的解决方案，因为你可以在任何地方玩这个游戏。”

她还指出，安全问题很少，因为用户可以通过镜片看到他们的“真实”环境，这与虚拟现实头显不同，后者用户只能看到游戏。

尽管到目前为止，这项技术的广泛实施一直受制于成本，D'Andrea表示乐观，认为该技术可能很快会更广泛地普及。

“我认为增强现实头显的价格终于开始下降，这可能会加速该技术在康复领域的应用，”她说。
