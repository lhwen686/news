Original Text
Every 1% increase in military spending drives a 0·62% reduction in public health spending.1 This trade-off is more intense in low-income countries, where a 1% increase in military spending results in a 0·962% drop in health spending.1 As global defence budgets surge to historical highs amid escalating conflicts in the Middle East, Ukraine, and beyond, this is not an abstract equation; it is a daily reality for the one in six people worldwide now living under active conflict.2 Evidence from 1990 to 2017 links conflict to an estimated 29·4 million excess deaths from indirect causes alone, such as disrupted health services.
