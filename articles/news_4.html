Study links low birthweight to increased stroke risk in young adults, independent of BMI and gestational age

这项研究揭示了低出生体重（LBW）作为成年早期卒中的一个独立风险因素，具有重要的临床和公共卫生意义。传统的卒中风险评估主要关注后天因素，如高血压、糖尿病、肥胖及吸烟等，但此研究强调了生命早期经历（如宫内发育）对晚期健康结果的深远影响。低出生体重可能反映了胎儿期营养不良或宫内环境不佳，这可能导致血管结构异常、内皮功能障碍或神经血管单元发育缺陷，从而增加血管脆性或血栓形成倾向，为成年后的缺血性或出血性卒中埋下伏笔。

研究结果独立于成年早期BMI和出生胎龄，这一点尤为关键。它表明，即使是足月出生且成年后体重正常的个体，若出生体重偏低，其卒中风险仍较高。这提示我们，除了预防早产和控制成年期肥胖，改善孕期健康和胎儿发育条件，以减少低出生体重儿的发生，也可能成为未来预防成年早期卒中的重要策略。在临床实践中，低出生体重信息可作为一项简单的生物标记，指导医生对相关个体进行更早期的风险筛查和个性化预防性干预，从而降低这一日益增长的年轻成人卒中发病率。

In new research to be presented at this year's European Congress on Obesity (ECO2026) in Istanbul, Turkey (12–15 May), researchers show an increased risk of stroke in young adulthood for children of low birthweight, independent of their body mass index (BMI) as young adults or gestational age at birth. The study of almost 800,000 people in Sweden suggests that low birthweight could be included in stroke risk assessment for adults, say the authors, who include Dr. Lina Lilja and Dr. Maria Bygdell of the University of Gothenburg, Gothenburg, Sweden, and colleagues.

While the incidence of stroke overall has declined in high income countries over the past decades, this decline has been less pronounced among young and middle-aged adults compared to older adults. In some regions and in these younger age groups, stroke incidence is even rising—including lower-income countries of southeast Asia and Oceania, and high-income countries including Sweden, the US, and the UK.

Thus, the authors decided to investigate whether factors such as birthweight, gestational age at birth, and BMI as a young adult could be risk factors for stroke in younger adults. While for young adults these life events have already passed, for children not yet conceived, these are factors that could be targeted with interventions.

Stroke types and study design

The authors investigated both common forms of stroke—ischemic, caused by a blockage of blood vessels in the brain (representing more than three-quarters of strokes, depending on location); and intercerebral hemorrhage (ICH/"brain bleed") strokes, representing up to a quarter of all strokes.

This population-based study included 420,173 men and 348,758 women born between 1973 and 1982 in Sweden (and thus aged 43 to 53 years if still alive today and representing all recorded live births from those years) with data on birth weight, gestational age, and BMI in young adulthood from the Medical Birth Register and the National Conscription register, respectively. Study participants were followed until December 31, 2022.

Information on early adult stroke events was retrieved from the National Patient Register and the Cause of Death Register in Sweden—2,252 first stroke events, mean age 36 years; 1,624 ischemic stroke [IS] events, mean age 37 years; and 588 intracerebral hemorrhage stroke [ICH] events, mean age 33 years (with 40 of these first stroke events uncategorized).

Findings on birthweight and risk

Since registration of diagnoses in these registers is mandatory, they provide comprehensive national data on individuals who have been hospitalized or died from stroke. The relatively low numbers of events reflect that these are younger adults, in whom absolute stroke risk is lower. All analyses were adjusted for gender, birth year, gestational age, parents' country of birth, and age at adult BMI.

The authors found that there was a 21% increased risk for all stroke events combined, as well as for ischemic stroke alone, and a 27% increased risk of intracerebral hemorrhage stroke alone, for individuals (men and women combined) having a birth weight below median (3.5 kg) compared to individuals having a birth weight above the median. Women with a birth weight below median (3.5 kg) had an 18% increased risk for all strokes combined, and men a 23% increased risk compared to individuals with a birth weight above median.

The results were independent of gestational age (how long the child had been in the womb before birth) and their BMI as a young adult. Gestational age was not itself associated with stroke risk. The overall results in each case were similar for women and men.

The authors conclude, "We demonstrate that lower birthweight is associated with an increased risk of early adult stroke. There is similar increased risk for both men and women and for both the major types of stroke, ischemic and hemorrhagic, and the results were independent of gestational age at birth, and BMI as young adults. These findings suggest that low birth weight may be included in assessments of stroke risk in adults."

在将于今年5月12日至15日在土耳其伊斯坦布尔举行的欧洲肥胖大会（ECO2026）上发表的新研究显示，低出生体重的儿童在成年早期卒中风险增加，且这种风险独立于其成年早期的身体质量指数（BMI）或出生时的胎龄。这项对瑞典近80万人进行的研究表明，低出生体重可被纳入成人的卒中风险评估中，研究作者称。作者团队包括瑞典哥德堡大学的Lina Lilja博士和Maria Bygdell博士及其同事。

尽管在过去几十年中，高收入国家卒中的总体发病率有所下降，但与老年人相比，这种下降在青年和中年成人中并不那么显著。在某些地区以及这些较年轻的年龄组中，卒中发病率甚至正在上升——这包括东南亚和大洋洲的低收入国家，以及瑞典、美国和英国等高收入国家。

因此，作者决定调查出生体重、出生胎龄以及青年时期的BMI等因素是否可能是年轻成人卒中的风险因素。对于已经进入青年期的成人来说，这些生命事件已经发生；而对于尚未受孕的儿童，这些因素则可能成为干预的目标。

卒中类型与研究设计

作者调查了两种常见的卒中类型——缺血性卒中，由脑部血管阻塞引起（根据位置不同，占卒中的四分之三以上）；以及脑内出血（ICH/“脑溢血”）卒中，占所有卒中的四分之一左右。

这项基于人群的研究纳入了1973年至1982年间在瑞典出生的420,173名男性和348,758名女性（因此，如果今天仍然健在，他们的年龄在43至53岁之间，代表了这些年份所有有记录的活产儿），其出生体重、胎龄和青年时期BMI数据分别来自医疗出生登记册和国家征兵登记册。研究参与者被追踪至2022年12月31日。

早期成人卒中事件的信息来自瑞典国家患者登记册和死因登记册——共2,252例首次卒中事件，平均年龄36岁；其中1,624例为缺血性卒中[IS]事件，平均年龄37岁；588例为脑内出血卒中[ICH]事件，平均年龄33岁（其中40例首次卒中事件未分类）。

出生体重与风险发现

由于在这些登记册中诊断登记是强制性的，因此它们提供了关于因卒中住院或死亡个体的全面全国数据。事件数量相对较低反映了这些是年轻成人，他们的绝对卒中风险较低。所有分析均根据性别、出生年份、胎龄、父母出生国和成人BMI时的年龄进行了调整。

作者发现，对于出生体重低于中位数（3.5公斤）的个体（男性和女性合计），所有卒中事件合计的风险增加了21%，缺血性卒中单独的风险也增加了21%，而脑内出血卒中单独的风险则增加了27%，均与出生体重高于中位数的个体相比。出生体重低于中位数（3.5公斤）的女性，所有卒中合计的风险增加了18%，男性则增加了23%，均与出生体重高于中位数的个体相比。

研究结果独立于胎龄（即孩子出生前在子宫内停留的时间）及其青年时期的BMI。胎龄本身与卒中风险无关。在每种情况下，男性和女性的总体结果相似。

作者总结道：“我们证明，较低的出生体重与成年早期卒中风险增加相关。男性和女性以及两种主要卒中类型（缺血性和出血性）的风险增加相似，且研究结果独立于出生胎龄和青年时期BMI。这些发现表明，低出生体重可被纳入成人卒中风险评估中。”
