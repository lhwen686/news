A new, more life-like physical model of microscopic nerve fibers called axons could speed up the discovery of medicines for multiple sclerosis and other degenerative brain diseases, suggests a new study led by UCL researchers. Testing drugs on an artificial replica that better matches the physical properties of human axons can reduce the risk of drugs that appear to perform well in the laboratory but ultimately fail in human trials, the researchers said.

In multiple sclerosis (MS), the immune system mistakenly attacks myelin, the protective sheath or insulation around axons—the long nerve fibers modeled in this study and responsible for carrying electrical signals in the brain and spinal cord.

A softer, more realistic axon model

The new model, described in a paper published in Nature Methods, consists of tiny pillars tens of times thinner than a human hair that replicate these axons. Unlike previous models made of hard materials such as plastics, this new one is made of a water-filled gel (hydrogel) that can be adjusted to be as soft as real axons.

The research team grew myelin from human and animal cells around these pillars and added drugs proposed as therapies to restore or repair damaged myelin to see whether they promoted myelin growth. However, they found that drugs performed less well when the model axons were more life-like, that is, were adjusted to be as soft as the real-life versions—pointing to one reason why past drugs, tested on less life-like models, may have succeeded in the laboratory and then failed in humans.

First human cell myelin grown in lab

The study also marks the first time that myelin has been successfully grown from human cells in the laboratory. Senior author Professor Emad Moeendarbary, based at UCL Mechanical Engineering, said, "To stop MS, we need therapies that repair myelin. Promising drug candidates in the past have failed when tested in human patients. One factor might be that laboratory models do not replicate the basic physical properties of the human brain. Another is that animal brains differ from human brains in so-called white matter, regions densely packed with myelinated axons.

"Our work suggests that commonly used rigid models, hundreds of times stiffer than real axons, can generate misleading drug hits. We believe that our more life-like model can be used as a more robust early test of drug candidates and as a platform to discover new drugs."

Why myelin repair matters in disease

The decline in the brain's ability to protect nerve fibers by restoring and repairing myelin is a key part of neurodegenerative diseases such as MS, as well as Alzheimer's, Parkinson's and motor neuron disease.

Myelin is repaired or replaced by special cells in the brain called oligodendrocytes. Early on in MS, this process continues to work, but with age and repeated attacks, the process stops working effectively.

The axons they protect carry messages that control everything we do—from how we move and think to how we feel. When the myelin gets damaged, these signals slow down or get lost, and in severe cases the unprotected neurons die.

Engineering the tiny hydrogel micropillars

For the study, the researchers engineered the model axons, called micropillars, using a micro-fabrication technique known as photolithography to create tiny molds, which were then filled with hydrogel to form the pillars.

They added human- and rodent-derived oligodendrocytes to the resulting micropillars and analyzed how many layers of myelin these cells produced around the pillars, and how this varied according to factors including the pillars' softness and the presence (or not) of drugs proposed as therapies to encourage myelin growth.

Professor Moeendarbary, who is an expert in cellular mechanics, added, "Hydrogel is a close mimic of living cells. Like an actual cell, it is made mostly of water and is porous. But to fabricate a soft hydrogel at such a small scale is not an easy task. The work was carried out mainly by Ph.D. student Soufian Lasli and Dr. Claire Vinel involved five years of careful experimentation and refinement. The result is a model that is as close as possible to real nerve fibers. We hope this can lead to more reliable validation of potential drugs before they reach patients in clinical trials."

一项由伦敦大学学院研究人员领导的新研究表明，一种新的、更接近真实的微观神经纤维（称为轴突）物理模型，有望加速多发性硬化症及其他神经退行性脑部疾病药物的发现。研究人员表示，在更符合人类轴突物理特性的仿真模型上测试药物，可以降低那些在实验室表现良好但在人体临床试验中最终失败的药物风险。

在多发性硬化症（MS）中，免疫系统错误地攻击髓鞘，髓鞘是轴突——本研究中建模的长神经纤维，负责在大脑和脊髓中传递电信号——周围的保护性髓鞘或绝缘层。

一个更柔软、更真实的轴突模型

这个在《自然方法》杂志上发表的新模型，由比人类头发细数十倍的微小柱体组成，它们复制了这些轴突。与以往由塑料等硬质材料制成的模型不同，这个新模型由水凝胶制成，可以调整得像真实轴突一样柔软。

研究团队围绕这些微柱体培养了来自人类和动物细胞的髓鞘，并加入了旨在恢复或修复受损髓鞘的药物候选物，以观察它们是否促进了髓鞘生长。然而，他们发现，当模型轴突更接近真实——即被调整得像真实版本一样柔软时——药物的效果反而较差。这揭示了一个原因，即为什么过去在不那么真实的模型上测试成功的药物，最终会在人体试验中失败。

首次在实验室中培养出人类细胞髓鞘

这项研究也标志着髓鞘首次成功地从人类细胞中在实验室培养出来。伦敦大学学院机械工程系的资深作者埃马德·莫恩达巴里教授表示：“要阻止多发性硬化症，我们需要修复髓鞘的疗法。过去有希望的药物候选物在人体患者中测试时失败了。一个因素可能是实验室模型未能复制人脑的基本物理特性。另一个原因是，动物大脑与人脑在所谓的白质（即髓鞘化轴突密集区域）方面存在差异。”

“我们的工作表明，常用的刚性模型比真实轴突硬数百倍，可能会产生误导性的药物阳性结果。我们相信，我们更接近真实的模型可以作为药物候选物更可靠的早期测试平台，并作为发现新药物的平台。”

为什么髓鞘修复对疾病很重要

大脑通过恢复和修复髓鞘来保护神经纤维的能力下降，是多发性硬化症等神经退行性疾病以及阿尔茨海默病、帕金森病和运动神经元疾病的关键部分。

髓鞘由大脑中称为少突胶质细胞的特殊细胞修复或替代。在多发性硬化症早期，这个过程仍然有效，但随着年龄增长和反复发作，这个过程就会停止有效工作。

它们保护的轴突携带控制我们一切行为的信号——从我们如何运动、思考到我们如何感受。当髓鞘受损时，这些信号会减慢或丢失，在严重情况下，未受保护的神经元会死亡。

微型水凝胶微柱的工程设计

在这项研究中，研究人员使用一种名为光刻的微加工技术，设计了被称为微柱的模型轴突，以制造微小的模具，然后将模具填充水凝胶以形成这些柱体。

他们将来自人类和啮齿动物的少突胶质细胞添加到所得的微柱体上，并分析了这些细胞围绕柱体产生髓鞘的层数，以及这如何根据包括柱体柔软度和旨在促进髓鞘生长的药物（或不含药物）等因素而变化。

莫恩达巴里教授是一位细胞力学专家，他补充道：“水凝胶与活细胞非常相似。像真正的细胞一样，它主要由水组成并且具有多孔性。但在如此小的尺度上制造柔软的水凝胶并非易事。这项工作主要由博士生苏菲安·拉斯利和克莱尔·维内尔博士完成，耗时五年精心实验和完善。结果是一个尽可能接近真实神经纤维的模型。我们希望这能在潜在药物进入患者临床试验之前，实现更可靠的验证。”
