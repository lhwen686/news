Spatial atlas reveals unique coordination among cell types that support healthy human skin

Mount Sinai researchers have published the first organ-wide human skin spatial atlas from across the body. It provides an unprecedentedly detailed view of healthy human skin, revealing cellular composition and functional mechanisms of skin from more than a dozen unique sites on the body, including the scalp and sole.

The skin is the largest human organ and plays a critical role in regulating body temperature and protecting the immune system as an external barrier. The skin is also susceptible to chronic disease, yet how the skin's cellular composition and molecular organization vary spatially across the entire body remains largely undefined.

Building a human skin atlas

In this landmark study, published in Nature Genetics on March 23, 2026, Mount Sinai experts constructed a comprehensive mapping of more than 1 million cells from normal adult human skin to identify 45 unique cell types that vary across composition and arrangement in the body. The researchers said this extensive mapping of critical skin cell function brings scientists one step closer to developing more targeted therapeutics that could potentially treat skin diseases like eczema and psoriasis, and restore function to core components of human skin.

Human skin diseases often occur in specific sites; for example, psoriasis frequently appears on the elbows, knees, and scalp. Cells in each region of the skin organize themselves into biological networks composed of diverse cell types that represent communication hubs, working together to maintain immune surveillance or detect pathogens, damaged cells, or early tumors on the skin to eliminate these threats. These systems, otherwise referred to as "multicellular neighborhoods," are abnormal or impaired when a patient has a skin disease and requires treatment.

Single-cell spatial transcriptomic profiling of adult human skin using MERFISH. Credit: Nature Genetics (2026). DOI: 10.1038/s41588-026-02552-8

Mapping multicellular skin neighborhoods

In this study, the Mount Sinai team aimed to identify the key molecular factors of these multicellular neighborhoods. The researchers collectively profiled more than 1.2 million cells across 15 anatomic sites from 22 donors to create a skin spatial atlas, a high-resolution map of the location of every individual cell across different layers in healthy human skin across the body. The researchers defined site-specific cell type composition and their organization into 10 recurrent multicellular neighborhoods that vary in their abundance across the body.

One particular neighborhood was composed of immune cells such as dendritic cells and T cells, which work together to train the immune system to attack specific threats, and was located around the skin's blood vessels. This "perivascular neighborhood" also contained key non-immune cell types, such as a specialized subset of connective tissue cells known as fibroblasts, that are critical for organizing the immune cell positions. The team noted that this perivascular neighborhood is reminiscent of specialized lymphoid tissues in other organs—like tonsils, which are trained to immunologically monitor the oral cavity—and could be considered the skin-associated lymphoid tissue. Within the perivascular neighborhood, the researchers identified the central role of tumor necrosis factor, a chemical messenger known to induce inflammation but which in the context of healthy skin maintains the specialized fibroblasts, highlighting essential communication between structural cells and immune cells.

In a comparison of molecular changes throughout various sites across the body and how neighborhoods are altered by diseases, the researchers noted tissue and organ impairment in the perivascular neighborhood, suggesting cellular alterations when there is disease activity, and thus a potential therapeutic target. The researchers concluded that multicellular neighborhoods underlie the skin's molecular and structural organization, coordinate cell-to-cell interactions, guide function, and exhibit structural disruption when a disease is present.

Zooming in with spatial transcriptomics

One of the key technological breakthroughs enabling this study is a new method called spatial transcriptomics, which allows high-throughput profiling of hundreds to thousands of genes that are pinpointed to their exact locations within individual cells. By using spatial transcriptomics to profile human skin, the researchers achieved an unprecedented view of the skin from a bird's-eye level down to the smallest genetic detail, comparable to smartphone map apps that allow one to zoom in from a country down to a city block.

"We reasoned that we need to understand what healthy skin looks like in order to fully understand human skin diseases," said corresponding author Andrew L. Ji, MD, Assistant Professor of Dermatology and a leading researcher in the Black Family Stem Cell Institute, and Kimberly and Eric J. Waldman Melanoma and Skin Cancer Center.

"By building this 'Google Maps' of skin across the entire body, we found higher-order organization involving these multicellular neighborhoods, wherein diverse cells prefer to be located next to one another. These cell types communicate with each other to maintain healthy function, such as establishing a barrier to the outside world.

"These neighborhoods can be thought of as the key building blocks of human skin, and we provide the blueprint for how they are constructed and how many comprise each body site. We envision that this atlas will serve as a global foundation, inviting researchers worldwide to layer on new types of data to reveal the full complexities of human skin."

Laying groundwork for future therapies

The discovery of unique cell types and spatial organization in human skin by the Mount Sinai team lays the groundwork for future studies and development of new treatment options for skin diseases, such as tissue engineering and stem-cell therapies, possibly restoring core components of healthy human skin.

New York University and Karolinska Institute in Sweden contributed to the research.

西奈山研究人员发布了首个涵盖全身范围的人体皮肤空间图谱。它以前所未有的详细程度展示了健康人体皮肤，揭示了来自身体十多个独特部位（包括头皮和足底）的皮肤细胞组成和功能机制。

皮肤是人体最大的器官，作为一道外部屏障，在调节体温和保护免疫系统方面发挥着关键作用。皮肤也容易受到慢性疾病的影响，然而，皮肤的细胞组成和分子组织如何在全身范围内空间性地变化，在很大程度上仍未明确。

构建人体皮肤图谱

在2026年3月23日发表于《自然·遗传学》的这项里程碑式研究中，西奈山专家构建了一个包含超过100万个正常成人人体皮肤细胞的综合图谱，识别出45种独特的细胞类型，这些细胞类型在全身的组成和排列上各不相同。研究人员表示，这种对关键皮肤细胞功能的广泛绘制，使科学家们离开发更具靶向性的疗法更近一步，这些疗法有望治疗湿疹和银屑病等皮肤疾病，并恢复人体皮肤核心组分的功能。

人类皮肤疾病常发生于特定部位；例如，银屑病常出现于肘部、膝盖和头皮。皮肤每个区域的细胞会组织成由不同细胞类型组成的生物网络，这些网络代表着通信枢纽，协同工作以维持免疫监视，或检测皮肤上的病原体、受损细胞或早期肿瘤，从而消除这些威胁。这些系统，也被称为“多细胞群落”，在患者患有皮肤疾病并需要治疗时会表现出异常或受损。

使用MERFISH技术对成人人体皮肤进行单细胞空间转录组分析。图片来源：《自然·遗传学》(2026)。DOI: 10.1038/s41588-026-02552-8

绘制皮肤多细胞群落图谱

在这项研究中，西奈山团队旨在识别这些多细胞群落的关键分子因子。研究人员总共对来自22名捐赠者的15个解剖部位的超过120万个细胞进行了分析，以创建一个皮肤空间图谱——一张高分辨率的地图，显示了健康人体皮肤不同层中每个单个细胞在全身范围内的位置。研究人员定义了部位特异性的细胞类型组成及其组织方式，形成了10个在全身丰度各不相同的重复性多细胞群落。

一个特定的群落由免疫细胞组成，例如树突状细胞和T细胞，它们协同工作以训练免疫系统攻击特定威胁，并位于皮肤血管周围。这个“血管周围群落”还包含关键的非免疫细胞类型，例如称为成纤维细胞的特殊结缔组织细胞亚群，它们对于组织免疫细胞的位置至关重要。该团队指出，这个血管周围群落让人联想到其他器官中的特化淋巴组织——比如扁桃体，其功能是免疫监测口腔——因此可以被视为皮肤相关淋巴组织。在血管周围群落中，研究人员识别出肿瘤坏死因子（一种已知能诱导炎症的化学信使）的核心作用，但在健康皮肤中，它维持着特化的成纤维细胞，这突显了结构细胞与免疫细胞之间必不可少的通信。

在比较全身各部位的分子变化以及疾病如何改变这些群落时，研究人员注意到血管周围群落存在组织和器官损伤，这表明在疾病活动时细胞会发生改变，从而提示了潜在的治疗靶点。研究人员总结道，多细胞群落是皮肤分子和结构组织的基础，它们协调细胞间的相互作用，指导功能，并在疾病存在时表现出结构性破坏。

空间转录组学提供微观视角

促成这项研究的关键技术突破之一是一种名为空间转录组学的新方法，它能够对数百到数千个基因进行高通量分析，并精确定位它们在单个细胞内的确切位置。通过使用空间转录组学分析人体皮肤，研究人员获得了前所未有的皮肤视图，从宏观层面一直到最小的基因细节，这类似于智能手机地图应用，可以从国家层面缩放到一个城市街区。

“我们认为，为了充分理解人类皮肤疾病，我们需要了解健康皮肤的样子，”皮肤病学助理教授、布莱克家族干细胞研究所及金伯利和埃里克·J·沃尔德曼黑色素瘤与皮肤癌中心的主要研究员、通讯作者Andrew L. Ji医学博士说。

“通过构建遍布全身的皮肤‘谷歌地图’，我们发现了涉及这些多细胞群落的高级组织，其中不同的细胞倾向于彼此相邻。这些细胞类型相互沟通，以维持健康功能，例如建立抵御外部世界的屏障。”

“这些群落可以被视为人体皮肤的关键组成部分，我们提供了它们如何构建以及每个身体部位由多少个群落组成的蓝图。我们设想，这个图谱将成为一个全球性的基础，邀请世界各地的研究人员在其上叠加新的数据类型，以揭示人体皮肤的全部复杂性。”

为未来疗法奠定基础

西奈山团队在人体皮肤中发现独特的细胞类型和空间组织，为未来的研究和开发皮肤疾病新治疗方案（如组织工程和干细胞疗法）奠定了基础，有望恢复健康人体皮肤的核心组分。

纽约大学和瑞典卡罗林斯卡学院对这项研究做出了贡献。
