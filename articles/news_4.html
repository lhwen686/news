Research links South Australian algal bloom to widespread eco-anxiety and distress

The year-long algal bloom along the South Australian coastline has not only devastated marine life and triggered health risks for humans and pets; it has also had a significant psychological impact on local residents, according to new research.

An Adelaide University survey of more than 600 South Australians during the peak of the 2025 bloom found high levels of "eco-anxiety"—distress linked to environmental damage—with many participants reporting persistent worry, sadness and feelings of helplessness.

The findings have been published in the Journal of Environmental Psychology.

Lead researcher Dr. Brianna Le Busque, Program Director of Environmental Science and Geospatial Science at Adelaide University, said the findings show marine environmental crises can have profound effects on community well-being.

"More than 60% of the people we surveyed said they were repeatedly thinking about the bloom or felt that their personal actions wouldn't make a difference," Dr. Le Busque said.

"Over half reported feeling anxious or afraid, and one in five said it was affecting their sleep or ability to concentrate. This tells us that the impacts of environmental crises extend well beyond the visible damage to ecosystems."

The survey captured responses from 613 residents while the bloom was actively unfolding. In the two weeks prior to completing the survey:

69% experienced "frequent rumination," meaning they couldn't stop thinking about the bloom

63% felt anxiety about personal impacts or helplessness

54% reported emotional symptoms such as fear or distress

20% experienced behavioral disruptions, including sleep difficulties.

Women reported significantly higher levels of eco-anxiety than men, and people who visited the ocean more often were also more likely to report distress.

Importantly, the bloom disrupted people's connection to the ocean—a place that many rely on for relaxation and mental well-being. Nearly half of those who said they were directly impacted reported stopping recreational activities such as swimming, surfing, walking or fishing.

"For many South Australians, the beach is a place of solace and restoration," Dr. Le Busque said. "When that space becomes associated with dead marine life, foam and health warnings, it changes how people feel about going there."

Just under 40% of directly affected participants said their mental health had suffered, describing feelings of grief, frustration and depression. Some compared seeing washed-up marine life to "the death of a loved one." A third said their distress was specifically linked to witnessing dead animals along the shoreline.

The study also identified physical health concerns, with 24% reporting issues such as coughing or respiratory irritation after visiting affected beaches.

Dr. Le Busque said the research highlights the need to recognize eco-anxiety as a legitimate public health concern—particularly as climate-driven marine events, including heat waves and algal blooms, become more frequent.

"We often think about the economic and environmental costs of events like harmful algal blooms," she said. "But we also need to consider the human cost. These events can disrupt people's daily routines, their sense of place, and their relationship with nature."

The researchers say understanding these psychological impacts is crucial for building community resilience and ensuring mental health support is part of future environmental response planning.

"This bloom has shown that when the ocean suffers, communities suffer too," Dr. Le Busque said.

最新研究显示，南澳大利亚海岸线长达一年的藻华不仅摧毁了海洋生物，并对人类和宠物造成了健康风险；它还对当地居民产生了显著的心理影响。

阿德莱德大学在2025年藻华高峰期对600多名南澳大利亚居民进行的一项调查发现，受访者普遍存在高水平的“生态焦虑”——一种与环境破坏相关的痛苦——许多参与者报告了持续的担忧、悲伤和无助感。

这些发现已发表在《环境心理学杂志》上。

主要研究员、阿德莱德大学环境科学与地理空间科学项目主任布丽安娜·勒·比斯克博士表示，这些发现表明海洋环境危机可能对社区福祉产生深远影响。

勒·比斯克博士说：“我们调查的人群中，超过60%的人表示他们反复思考藻华问题，或者觉得他们的个人行动不会产生任何改变。”

“超过一半的人报告感到焦虑或恐惧，五分之一的人表示这影响了他们的睡眠或注意力集中能力。这表明环境危机的影响远远超出了对生态系统的可见损害。”

该调查在藻华活跃期收集了613名居民的回复。在完成调查前的两周内：

69%的人经历了“频繁反刍思维”，这意味着他们无法停止思考藻华。

63%的人对个人影响感到焦虑或无助。

54%的人报告了恐惧或痛苦等情绪症状。

20%的人经历了行为障碍，包括睡眠困难。

女性报告的生态焦虑水平显著高于男性，而更频繁前往海洋的人也更有可能报告痛苦。

重要的是，藻华破坏了人们与海洋的联系——许多人依赖海洋进行放松和维持心理健康。近一半自称直接受影响的人表示停止了游泳、冲浪、散步或钓鱼等休闲活动。

勒·比斯克博士说：“对许多南澳大利亚人来说，海滩是慰藉和恢复精神的地方。当这个空间与死亡的海洋生物、泡沫和健康警告联系在一起时，人们对去那里的感受就会发生改变。”

近40%的直接受影响参与者表示他们的心理健康受到了损害，他们描述了悲伤、沮丧和抑郁的感觉。有些人将看到冲上岸的海洋生物比作“失去一位挚爱”。三分之一的人表示，他们的痛苦尤其与目睹沿岸死亡动物有关。

该研究还发现了身体健康问题，24%的受访者报告在访问受影响海滩后出现咳嗽或呼吸道刺激等问题。

勒·比斯克博士表示，这项研究强调了将生态焦虑视为一个合法的公共卫生问题来认识的必要性——尤其是在气候驱动的海洋事件（包括热浪和藻华）变得更加频繁之际。

她说：“我们常常只考虑有害藻华等事件的经济和环境成本。但我们也需要考虑人类的代价。这些事件会扰乱人们的日常生活、他们的归属感以及他们与自然的关系。”

研究人员表示，了解这些心理影响对于建立社区韧性并确保将心理健康支持纳入未来的环境应对规划至关重要。

勒·比斯克博士说：“这次藻华事件表明，当海洋遭受苦难时，社区也会随之受苦。”
