从痴呆症到糖尿病：新工具追踪溶液中的无序蛋白

From dementia to diabetes: New tool tracks disordered proteins in solution

💡 AI 深度解析 (AI Insight)

本文详细阐述了一项在固有无序蛋白（IDP）分析领域的重大突破。IDP缺乏稳定的三维结构，但在细胞功能中至关重要，其动态特性使得研究极具挑战性。然而，IDP的错误折叠或聚集与阿尔茨海默病、帕金森病等神经退行性疾病及2型糖尿病等代谢紊乱密切相关。此次创新在于建立了一种“融合策略”，将人工智能驱动的计算模拟与高精度核磁共振（NMR）光谱实验数据相结合。具体而言，AI模型生成大量IDP结构候选，随后通过“最大熵”技术进行精炼，对与真实NMR数据吻合度最高的结构赋予更高权重，从而精确识别以前难以捕捉的瞬时中间结构。

这项技术具有深远的行业影响。通过精确表征IDP的构象集合，研究人员能够前所未有地深入理解IDP在疾病发病机制中原子级的错误折叠和聚集机制。这种理解是开发针对痴呆症等IDP相关难治疾病新型治疗靶点和药物的基础。此外，建立韩国版无定形蛋白质PDB的举措，强调了促进开放科学和加速结构生物学研究的承诺，预示着复杂蛋白质病变领域药物发现和个性化医疗的新纪元。

A DGIST research team led by Prof. Yoo Wookyung (Department of Brain Sciences) and Prof. Kim Jin Hae (Department of New Biology) developed an innovative analytical technology in collaboration with a research team led by Dr. Lee Young-ho from the Protein Structure/Interaction Research Group at the Korea Basic Science Institute (KBSI). This technology enables precise, atomic-level examination of intrinsically disordered proteins (IDP), which are difficult to analyze because of their lack of fixed forms. The study is published in the journal Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences.

While proteins are known to perform normal functions when they possess a well-defined three-dimensional structure, approximately one-third of human proteins are IDP, which change constantly without a specific structure or shape, much like a limp skein. These proteins play essential roles, such as delivering intracellular signals, but if they are abnormally misfolded or aggregated, they become a key cause of neurodegenerative diseases (e.g., Alzheimer's and Parkinson's diseases, which are represented by dementia) and metabolic disorders (e.g., type 2 diabetes). However, because of the ever-changing nature of their movements, a technical limitation has existed in elucidating the specific misfolding mechanisms involved in the development of various diseases.

To overcome the limitation, the research team established an optimized fusion strategy that combined computer simulation technology with actual experimental data. First, the team generated tens of thousands of structural candidates of proteins using an artificial intelligence (AI) model, advanced simulations, and structure-related information from the Protein Data Bank (PDB).

Next, the research team applied a "maximum entropy" technique to compare the candidates against actual nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR) data obtained from real experiments and assign higher weights to structures that are closest to the actual protein state. This technology's key feature is its ability to accurately identify even intermediate-level structures that proteins form for brief moments.

Notably, this achievement was made possible by the precise NMR experimental data provided by Dr. Lee Young-ho's research team at the KBSI. KBSI's precise NMR data facilitated the observation of protein structures at the atomic level in the solution state rather than in the solid or crystalline state, and played a decisive role in validating and providing feedback on whether the algorithm developed by the DGIST's research team accurately reflected the actual physical properties of proteins. Based on this, the research team tracked precisely how protein structures change depending on temperature or genetic mutations.

Prof. Yoo of DGIST stated, "This research is a valuable achievement resulting from the integration of DGIST's Supercomputing AI Education and Research Center's computational resources and advanced computational science technologies with KBSI's world-class precision analysis infrastructure and techniques.

"By unraveling the structural secrets of amorphous proteins, which were previously impossible to analyze, we expect this to become a crucial analytical tool for understanding the pathogeneses of intractable diseases, such as dementia, and for developing treatments to control them."

Dr. Lee of KBSI said, "Through ongoing collaboration with DGIST, we plan to continue developing structural research tools targeting amorphous and disease-related proteins.

"Together with the Korea Institute of Science and Technology Information (KISTI), we will establish a Korean version of PDB (PDBj in Korea) based on PDBj, one of the world's top three PDBs, to create an archive for the structures of amorphous proteins lacking fixed three-dimensional structures."

This research featured Jeon Joo-hyung, an integrated master's and Ph.D. candidate at DGIST, as the first author and listed DGIST professors Yoo Wookyung and Kim Jin Hae, as well as KBSI's Dr. Lee Young-ho, as co-corresponding authors.

由DGIST脑科学系柳佑敬教授和新生物学系金镇海教授领导的研究团队，与韩国基础科学研究院（KBSI）蛋白质结构/相互作用研究组的李英浩博士领导的研究团队合作，开发了一项创新的分析技术。这项技术能够对固有无序蛋白（IDP）进行精确的原子级检查，由于IDP缺乏固定形态，其分析一直非常困难。这项研究已发表在《美国国家科学院院刊》上。

蛋白质通常在具有明确的三维结构时发挥正常功能，然而，大约三分之一的人体蛋白质是固有无序蛋白（IDP），它们像一团松散的线团一样，不断变化而没有特定的结构或形状。这些蛋白质在传递细胞内信号等方面发挥着重要作用，但如果它们异常错误折叠或聚集，就会成为神经退行性疾病（如阿尔茨海默病和帕金森病，统称为痴呆症）和代谢紊乱（如2型糖尿病）的关键病因。然而，由于IDP运动的不断变化性，在阐明与各种疾病发生相关的具体错误折叠机制方面一直存在技术限制。

为了克服这一限制，研究团队建立了一种优化的融合策略，将计算机模拟技术与实际实验数据相结合。首先，该团队利用人工智能（AI）模型、先进模拟技术以及来自蛋白质数据库（PDB）的结构相关信息，生成了数万个蛋白质结构候选。

接下来，研究团队应用了一种“最大熵”技术，将这些候选结构与从实际实验中获得的核磁共振光谱（NMR）数据进行比较，并对最接近实际蛋白质状态的结构赋予更高的权重。这项技术的关键特点是它能够准确识别蛋白质短暂形成的中间状态结构。

值得注意的是，这一成就得益于KBSI李英浩博士研究团队提供的精确核磁共振（NMR）实验数据。KBSI的精确NMR数据有助于在溶液状态而非固态或晶态下观察蛋白质的原子级结构，并在验证和反馈DGIST研究团队开发的算法是否准确反映蛋白质实际物理性质方面发挥了决定性作用。在此基础上，研究团队精确追踪了蛋白质结构如何随温度或基因突变而变化。

DGIST的柳教授表示：“这项研究是DGIST超级计算人工智能教育研究中心的计算资源和先进计算科学技术与KBSI世界级精密分析基础设施及技术相结合的宝贵成果。”

“通过揭示以前无法分析的无定形蛋白质的结构秘密，我们期望这成为理解痴呆症等难治疾病发病机制并开发控制其治疗方法的关键分析工具。”

KBSI的李博士表示：“通过与DGIST的持续合作，我们计划继续开发针对无定形和疾病相关蛋白质的结构研究工具。”

“我们将与韩国科学技术信息研究院（KISTI）合作，基于全球三大PDB之一的PDBj，建立一个韩国版PDB（PDBj in Korea），以创建缺乏固定三维结构的无定形蛋白质的结构档案。”

这项研究由DGIST硕士博士联合培养候选人Jeon Joo-hyung担任第一作者，DGIST的柳佑敬教授和金镇海教授，以及KBSI的李英浩博士共同担任通讯作者。
