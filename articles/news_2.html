Six-week virtual program offers early palliative care roadmap for dementia

For an estimated 11% of Americans over age 65 who have dementia and the over 11 million unpaid caregivers supporting them, there is no instruction manual for navigating life after diagnosis. A team of College of Nursing researchers at the Medical University of South Carolina is working to fill that gap. The team is led by Diana Layne, Ph.D., R.N., assistant professor, and her mentor, Teresa Kelechi, Ph.D., associate dean for Research.

Layne brings her expertise in dementia research to the team, and her KL2 award from the South Carolina Clinical & Translational Research Institute (SCTR) made this research possible. Kelechi, who serves as director of recruitment at SCTR, brings a rich background in geriatrics and dementia caregiver support.

Their article, published in Palliative and Supportive Care, introduces a new structured virtual palliative care program—SUPPORT-D—designed to serve as a roadmap for the course of the illness.

The six-week program, adapted from a pulmonary fibrosis intervention introduced by Kathleen Lindell, Ph.D., R.N., the Mary Swain Endowed Chair in Palliative Care Health, includes a specially designed educational booklet and two meetings with a nurse interventionist. Patients and caregivers complete the structured course independently over several weeks, then meet with the nurse interventionist to personalize the program to their specific needs.

SUPPORT-D offers guided support in four main areas: understanding the disease, patient self-care, caregiver care, and planning for the future. By offering support early, the program helps patients and caregivers to reduce unnecessary burdens and improve quality of life.

Dementia is an umbrella term that describes symptoms such as memory loss, confusion, and difficulty with thinking and decision-making. Alzheimer's disease, while not the only type, is the most common form. Because dementia often has a long and complex course, a diagnosis affects not only the person with the disease but also caregivers and family members.

To address the challenges of the progressive condition, Layne's program centers on palliative care. Often confused with hospice care, which is meant for end of life, palliative care can begin at diagnosis and can include curative treatments. It focuses on symptom management, emotional support, decision-making, and quality of life.

"Palliative care is not something to fear," said Layne. "It's an extra layer of support."

Providing the targeted SUPPORT-D strategies meets a need that current dementia treatments often do not address. Shortages of neurologists can delay early diagnosis.

Primary care visits are often too short to allow for comprehensive counseling, and families frequently leave appointments without clear guidance. Many turn to the internet for answers, which can increase fear and confusion.

Strong evidence suggests that families want guidance on what to expect, but health care systems are not always set up to provide it. "We have been leaving them to their own devices without the information they needed to make the best possible choices," explained Layne.

Both Layne and Kelechi understand the limitations of dementia support firsthand.

Layne began studying dementia through mentorship and her passion for palliative care, only to become a caregiver for both of her in-laws after they developed cognitive decline. She recently moved them into her home. "I was living everything my participants were talking about, and I was still struggling to navigate the system," she said. "If I'm struggling, I can't imagine what happens to people who don't have that baseline knowledge."

Informed by her own experiences, professional and personal alike, Kelechi empathizes with the plight of the caregiver and the difficulty of navigating care. "It's a frightening, lonely experience to make decisions that could affect someone financially and emotionally," she said. Her understanding of dementia has fueled her previous work and has culminated in this program. "I never gave up on making caregiving better," said Kelechi.

The results of this Phase I feasibility trial speak to the team's commitment to constant improvement in dementia education and care. Seventy-six percent of participants completed the program, and most reported that the intervention was helpful.

Participants used the booklet regularly and even brought it to medical appointments to guide their discussions with providers. Most noted that it was helpful in understanding the disease and planning for their future.

Feedback from participants showed a desire for more support with stress management. A prior study by Kelechi found that mindfulness practices, gentle yoga, and breathing movements can help to reduce anxiety.

She and Layne plan to make these approaches a focus of future versions of the program. Their next step is to apply for funding, which would allow the team to expand SUPPORT-D with additional elements requested by patients and caregivers after the study. "We'll give them a menu," said Kelechi. "And they can pick what works best for them."

SUPPORT-D shows that early palliative care for patients with dementia is not only possible, but it is welcomed. Families want guidance, structure, and support at the beginning of the disease journey. By introducing planning early, this program empowers patients while they can still take part in decisions and reduces caregiver stress before a crisis hits.

The study lays the groundwork for larger clinical trials and signals a shift toward making palliative care a routine, supportive part of managing dementia, not something reserved for the final stages of life.

"Making palliative care a common language in primary care can really improve quality of life," said Layne. "Early palliative care works, and families want it."

对于美国估计有11%的65岁以上痴呆症患者以及支持他们的1100多万无偿照护者而言，在确诊后如何应对生活并没有一本指导手册。南卡罗来纳医科大学护理学院的一个研究团队正在努力填补这一空白。该团队由助理教授、注册护士戴安娜·莱恩博士和她的导师、研究副院长特蕾莎·凯莱奇博士领导。

莱恩将她在痴呆症研究方面的专业知识带到团队中，她从南卡罗来纳临床与转化研究中心（SCTR）获得的KL2奖项使得这项研究得以实现。凯莱奇则担任SCTR的招聘总监，她在老年医学和痴呆症照护者支持方面拥有丰富的背景。

他们发表在《姑息与支持护理》期刊上的文章介绍了一种新的结构化虚拟姑息护理计划——SUPPORT-D，旨在作为疾病全程的路线图。

这个为期六周的计划，借鉴了由姑息护理健康玛丽·斯温捐赠讲席教授、注册护士凯瑟琳·林德尔博士引入的肺纤维化干预措施，其中包括一本专门设计的教育手册和两次与护士干预师的会面。患者和照护者在数周内独立完成结构化课程，然后与护士干预师会面，以便根据其具体需求个性化该计划。

SUPPORT-D在四个主要方面提供指导性支持：了解疾病、患者自我照护、照护者照护以及未来规划。通过早期提供支持，该计划帮助患者和照护者减轻不必要的负担并提高生活质量。

痴呆症是一个涵盖性术语，描述记忆力丧失、意识模糊以及思维和决策困难等症状。阿尔茨海默病虽然不是唯一类型，但却是最常见的形式。由于痴呆症的病程往往漫长而复杂，诊断不仅影响患者本人，还影响照护者和家庭成员。

为应对这种进行性疾病的挑战，莱恩的计划以姑息护理为中心。姑息护理常与临终关怀混淆，后者仅适用于生命末期，而姑息护理可以从诊断时开始，并可包括治愈性治疗。它侧重于症状管理、情感支持、决策制定和生活质量。

“姑息护理不是需要恐惧的东西，”莱恩说，“它是一种额外的支持。”

提供有针对性的SUPPORT-D策略，满足了当前痴呆症治疗常未解决的需求。神经科医生短缺可能导致早期诊断延迟。

初级保健门诊时间通常过短，无法进行全面咨询，家庭常常在就诊后仍缺乏明确的指导。许多人转向互联网寻求答案，这可能增加恐惧和困惑。

强有力的证据表明，家庭希望获得对未来预期的指导，但医疗系统并非总能提供。莱恩解释说：“我们一直让他们自行其是，却没有获得做出最佳选择所需的信息。”

莱恩和凯莱奇都亲身了解痴呆症支持的局限性。

莱恩通过导师指导和对姑息护理的热情开始研究痴呆症，后来她的两位姻亲都出现了认知能力下降，她也成为了他们的照护者。她最近把他们接到了自己家中。“我亲身经历了我的参与者所谈论的一切，但我仍然在努力应对这个系统，”她说，“如果我都在挣扎，我无法想象那些没有基础知识的人会怎样。”

凯莱奇结合自己的专业和个人经验，对照护者的困境以及应对照护的困难感同身受。“做出可能在经济和情感上影响他人的决定，是一种令人恐惧而孤独的体验，”她说。她对痴呆症的理解推动了她以前的工作，并最终促成了这个计划。“我从未放弃让照护工作变得更好，”凯莱奇说。

这一I期可行性试验的结果证明了团队致力于不断改进痴呆症教育和照护的决心。76%的参与者完成了该计划，大多数人报告干预措施很有帮助。

参与者定期使用手册，甚至将其带到医疗预约中，以指导他们与医护人员的讨论。大多数人指出，这有助于他们了解疾病并规划未来。

参与者的反馈表明，他们渴望获得更多压力管理方面的支持。凯莱奇之前的一项研究发现，正念练习、温和瑜伽和呼吸运动有助于减轻焦虑。

她和莱恩计划将这些方法作为该计划未来版本的重点。他们的下一步是申请资金，这将使团队能够在研究结束后，根据患者和照护者的要求，扩展SUPPORT-D并增加额外元素。“我们会给他们一份‘菜单’，”凯莱奇说，“他们可以选择最适合自己的。”

SUPPORT-D表明，对痴呆症患者进行早期姑息护理不仅可行，而且受到欢迎。家庭在疾病旅程的开始阶段就希望获得指导、结构化和支持。通过早期引入规划，该计划在患者仍能参与决策时赋予他们力量，并在危机发生前减轻照护者的压力。

这项研究为更大规模的临床试验奠定了基础，并预示着姑息护理将从生命末期专享转变为痴呆症管理中常规、支持性的一部分。

“让姑息护理成为初级保健中的常用语言，确实可以改善生活质量，”莱恩说，“早期姑息护理有效，而且家庭需要它。”
