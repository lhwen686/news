Stay or stray? Why some gut microbes persist after fecal transplants

此项研究在粪便微生物群移植（FMT）领域取得了关键进展，揭示了供体肠道微生物成功定植于患者肠道的深层机制。核心在于，研究不再局限于简单的微生物物种鉴定，而是首次聚焦于“生物合成基因簇”——即指导细菌产生特定功能分子的基因群。这些基因簇的功能性产物在肠道微生态竞争与生存中扮演决定性角色。研究团队识别出两类基因簇：稳定的和短暂的。结果显示，携带稳定生物合成基因簇的供体微生物更易在受体肠道中实现持久性植入（平均76%），且这些稳定基因与益生菌的竞争和生存优势特质相关。

这一发现对临床实践和生物医药产业具有深远影响。它为改进FMT的“供体选择”提供了精准的遗传生物标志物，显著提升了FMT治疗（如艰难梭菌感染）的“可预测性”和“疗效”。更重要的是，该成果为“活体生物疗法”（LBT）的“合理设计”奠定了基础，使其从经验性疗法迈向精准医疗。未来，可以根据这些基因簇的功能特性，筛选、工程化或合成本土化益生菌株及其代谢产物，开发出更靶向、更安全且可扩展的新型微生物组疗法，甚至从中发现新的药物分子，以期应用于更广泛的胃肠道及全身性疾病的治疗。

Scientists have identified why some gut microbes successfully stay in the gut after fecal transplants, while others are much more transient. The King's College London discovery could help make the treatment—which involves transferring feces from a healthy donor into the gut of a patient—safer and more effective. The findings are published in the journal Gut Microbes.

Dr. Saeed Shoaie, a Reader in Systems and Synthetic Biology at King's College London and senior author of the study, said, "Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) can effectively modify gut microbial composition, but we have less reliable methods to predict which donor gut microbes will persist long enough to help patients."

Gut microbes are the billions of bacteria and other microscopic organisms like viruses and fungi that live in our digestive tract. Together they form what's known as the gut microbiome, which we know plays an important role in digestion, immunity and overall health.

In some people, reoccurring infections caused by a bacterium called Clostridioides difficile (C. diff) can severely disrupt the gut microbiome. The infection often takes hold after repeated antibiotic use, when healthy bacteria have been depleted.

How fecal transplants aim to help

Fecal microbiota transplantation can help restore a healthy balance of microbes in patients with C.diff infection, and is also being studied as a potential treatment for other conditions, including irritable bowel syndrome, Crohn's disease and even depression.

However, one of the major challenges is predicting which donor microbes will successfully stay in the gut of the patient long term.

Tracking bacterial genes over time

Now, an international team at King's and the Karolinska Institute in Sweden—whose team was led by Professor Adnane Achour—has tracked key genetic features of gut bacteria in 86 healthy adults over the course of a year.

Rather than simply focusing on which bacteria were present, the team examined biosynthetic gene clusters—or groups of genes—that instruct bacteria to produce certain molecules. These molecules help microbes compete with one another and influence which ones survive in the gut.

Dr. Fernando Guevara, a Research Associate in Translational Systems Biology at King's College London and first author of the study, said, "Analyzing gut bacteria based on what they produce could help to discover new medicines or better control infections."

Stable versus transient genetic features

They found that this group of genes falls into two distinct categories. One group was stable and consistently present over time, while the other only appeared sporadically.

When the researchers examined what happened during fecal transplantation, they found that the stable gene groups were far more likely to be present in patients long-term. On average, 76% of these stable genetic features present in donors were detected in patients after transplantation, compared with just 28% of the more transient ones.

Encouragingly, the stable gene groups were linked to traits that help good bacteria compete and survive, and less associated with genes linked to harmful behavior. This raises the possibility of using them as a source of future drugs inspired by bacteria already living in our bodies.

Previous research by King's suggests that the microbes most capable of long-term survival may also be much more helpful within the gut.

Dr. Shoaie added, "By identifying the genetic features that predict which donor microbes will successfully colonize the gut, we are one step closer to improving donor selection and designing therapies. This result can be a blueprint for live biotherapeutics (LBT), meaning rational design is possible and enabling more precise and scalable microbiome-based therapeutics."

科学家们已查明，为何有些肠道微生物在粪便移植后能成功留存于肠道，而另一些则更为短暂。伦敦国王学院的这项发现有助于使这项治疗——即将健康供体的粪便转移至患者肠道——变得更安全、更有效。研究结果已发表在《Gut Microbes》杂志上。

伦敦国王学院系统与合成生物学讲师、该研究的资深作者Saeed Shoaie博士表示：“粪便微生物群移植（FMT）能有效改变肠道微生物组成，但我们缺乏更可靠的方法来预测哪些供体肠道微生物能持续足够长时间以帮助患者。”

肠道微生物是生活在我们消化道中的数十亿细菌及其他微观生物，如病毒和真菌。它们共同构成了所谓的肠道微生物组，我们知道它在消化、免疫和整体健康中发挥着重要作用。

在某些人中，由艰难梭菌（C. diff）引起的反覆感染会严重扰乱肠道微生物组。这种感染常在反复使用抗生素后发生，此时健康细菌已被耗尽。

粪便移植如何提供帮助

粪便微生物群移植可以帮助艰难梭菌感染患者恢复健康的微生物平衡，目前也正在研究其作为其他疾病的潜在治疗方法，包括肠易激综合征、克罗恩病甚至抑郁症。

然而，主要挑战之一是预测哪些供体微生物能长期成功留存于患者肠道。

追踪细菌基因随时间的变化

现在，由Adnane Achour教授领导的伦敦国王学院和瑞典卡罗林斯卡学院的国际团队，在一年多的时间里追踪了86名健康成年人肠道细菌的关键遗传特征。

该团队没有简单地关注存在哪些细菌，而是检查了生物合成基因簇——即指导细菌产生特定分子的基因群。这些分子帮助微生物相互竞争，并影响哪些微生物能在肠道中存活。

伦敦国王学院转化系统生物学研究员、该研究的第一作者Fernando Guevara博士说：“基于细菌产生何种物质来分析肠道细菌，有助于发现新药物或更好地控制感染。”

稳定与短暂的遗传特征

他们发现这组基因分为两个截然不同的类别。一类是稳定的，并持续存在，而另一类则零星出现。

当研究人员检查粪便移植过程中发生的情况时，他们发现稳定的基因群更有可能长期存在于患者体内。平均而言，供体中存在的这些稳定遗传特征有76%在移植后在患者体内被检测到，而那些更短暂的遗传特征仅为28%。

令人鼓舞的是，这些稳定的基因群与有助于益生菌竞争和生存的特性相关，而与有害行为相关的基因关联较少。这提出了利用它们作为未来药物来源的可能性，这些药物的灵感来源于我们体内已有的细菌。

伦敦国王学院此前的研究表明，最能长期存活的微生物在肠道内也可能更有益。

Shoaie博士补充说：“通过识别预测哪些供体微生物能成功定植于肠道的遗传特征，我们离改进供体选择和设计疗法又近了一步。这一结果可以作为活体生物疗法（LBT）的蓝图，这意味着合理设计成为可能，并能实现更精确、可扩展的微生物组疗法。”
