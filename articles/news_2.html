肠道微生物能否影响肌肉力量？

Could a gut microbe influence muscle strength?

The trillions of microbes living in the human gut are increasingly recognized as important partners in human health. Scientists have linked the gut microbiome to several aspects of health, from metabolism and immunity to mental health.

A recent study suggests that these microbes may also influence an important aspect of fitness—muscle strength.

Muscle strength is a crucial feature of health for many reasons. It supports our joints and keeps our bones healthy, boosts athletic performance and even plays a role in metabolic health.

Muscle strength also helps us maintain independence later in life. As muscles gradually weaken as we get older, everyday tasks become harder and the risk of falls increases. Understanding what influences muscle strength is therefore an important part of healthy aging research.

A recent study explored whether specific gut bacteria might be linked to muscle strength. Researchers analyzed the gut microbiomes of two groups of adults: 90 young adults aged 18 to 25 and 33 older adults aged 65 to 75.

Participants provided stool samples so researchers could identify the microbes living in their gut. The researchers used DNA sequencing to read genetic material from the microbes in each sample. By comparing these sequences with large reference databases, they could determine which bacterial species were present and how abundant they were.

Participants also completed several tests designed to measure muscle strength, including a handgrip test. This involves squeezing a handheld device as hard as possible. Grip strength is widely used in health research because it provides a snapshot of overall muscle strength. Lower grip strength has also been linked to a higher risk of premature death.

When the researchers compared participants' muscle strength with the microbes in their gut, one species stood out. Higher levels of a bacterium called Roseburia inulinivorans were linked to stronger performance across muscle strength measures.

Finding a link like this is interesting, but it does not necessarily mean the microbe is responsible. Many things can be associated without one directly causing the other. Ice-cream sales and shark attacks both increase during summer, for example—but eating ice cream does not cause shark attacks.

So to investigate whether the bacterium might actually influence muscle strength, the researchers carried out additional experiments in mice. After reducing the animals' existing gut microbes, they introduced Roseburia inulinivorans into the mice's digestive systems.

Mice that received the bacterium developed noticeably stronger grip strength in their arms than those that did not. Their muscle fibers also became larger and shifted toward a type of fiber associated with more powerful movements (called type II muscle fibers).

Further analysis suggested Roseburia inulinivorans may influence how muscles use energy. In mice given R inulinivorans, several energy‑related pathways inside muscle cells became more active. At the same time, levels of certain amino acids (molecules used by all living things to make proteins) decreased in the gut and bloodstream.

The human data revealed another interesting pattern. Older adults in the study tended to have lower levels of Roseburia inulinivorans in their gut microbiome than the younger participants. This fits with the broader pattern of declining muscle strength that commonly occurs with age.

In humans, it's still unclear whether gut bacteria influence muscle strength or whether stronger, more active people simply have different microbes in their gut. But the mouse experiments hint that this microbe can directly enhance muscle strength, so larger human studies will be needed to work out the direction of the relationship.

Muscle microbes

One possibility raised by this research is the future use of probiotics. These products contain live microbes intended to benefit health. If further studies confirm that Roseburia inulinivorans supports muscle strength in humans, it could be developed into a probiotic designed to help maintain muscle function as people age.

However, supplements are not the only way to encourage beneficial microbes in the gut. Diet plays a major role in shaping the microbiome.

Prebiotic fibers, which serve as food for gut bacteria, can also support their growth. This is because feeding these microbes allows them to become more established and active in the gut.

The name inulinivorans provides a clue about this bacterium's preferred food source. It refers to inulin, a type of dietary fiber found naturally in foods such as onions, garlic, leeks, asparagus and chicory root. These fibers are known to support the growth of other beneficial gut bacteria, including members of the Roseburia group.

High‑fiber diets have long been associated with a range of health benefits. A large amount of research has linked higher fiber intake with lower risks of heart disease, type 2 diabetes and some cancers. These effects are probably driven by the complex activity of many different microbes rather than a single species. So at the moment, supplementation of any one individual bacterium is not a replacement for a diet high in fiber.

The study does have some limitations to note, however. The human groups were relatively small, and the experiments demonstrating cause and effect were conducted in mice rather than people. The older adults included in the study were also all male. Even so, the findings add to growing evidence that the gut microbiome may influence far more aspects of health than previously thought.

For now, the advice for supporting both muscle strength and a healthy microbiome remains reassuringly familiar: regular strength‑building exercise and a diet rich in fiber.

生活在人体肠道中的数万亿微生物正日益被认为是人类健康的重要伙伴。科学家们已将肠道微生物组与健康的多个方面联系起来，从新陈代谢和免疫力到心理健康。

最近一项研究表明，这些微生物可能还会影响身体素质的一个重要方面——肌肉力量。

肌肉力量是健康的一个关键特征，原因有许多。它支撑着我们的关节，保持骨骼健康，提升运动表现，甚至在新陈代谢健康中也发挥作用。

肌肉力量还有助于我们晚年保持独立性。随着年龄增长，肌肉逐渐衰弱，日常任务变得更加困难，跌倒的风险也随之增加。因此，了解影响肌肉力量的因素是健康老龄化研究的重要组成部分。

最近一项研究探讨了特定肠道细菌是否与肌肉力量有关。研究人员分析了两组成年人的肠道微生物组：90名18至25岁的年轻成年人，以及33名65至75岁的老年成年人。

参与者提供了粪便样本，以便研究人员识别其肠道中存在的微生物。研究人员使用DNA测序来读取每个样本中微生物的遗传物质。通过将这些序列与大型参考数据库进行比较，他们能够确定存在哪些细菌物种及其丰度。

参与者还完成了几项旨在测量肌肉力量的测试，包括握力测试。这需要尽可能用力挤压手持设备。握力在健康研究中被广泛使用，因为它能提供整体肌肉力量的概览。较低的握力也与较高的过早死亡风险相关。

当研究人员比较参与者的肌肉力量与他们肠道中的微生物时，一种物种脱颖而出。一种名为菊粉梭菌（Roseburia inulinivorans）的细菌水平较高，与各项肌肉力量测量指标中的更强表现相关。

发现这种联系很有趣，但这并不一定意味着这种微生物就是原因。许多事物之间可能存在关联，但并非一者直接导致另一者。例如，夏季冰淇淋销量和鲨鱼袭击事件都会增加，但吃冰淇淋并不会导致鲨鱼袭击。

因此，为了调查这种细菌是否真的会影响肌肉力量，研究人员在小鼠身上进行了额外实验。在减少动物体内已有的肠道微生物后，他们将菊粉梭菌引入小鼠的消化系统。

接受该细菌的小鼠，其胳膊的握力比未接受的小鼠显著增强。它们的肌肉纤维也变得更大，并转向与更强有力运动相关的纤维类型（称为II型肌纤维）。

进一步分析表明，菊粉梭菌可能影响肌肉如何利用能量。在接受菊粉梭菌的小鼠中，肌肉细胞内的一些能量相关通路变得更加活跃。与此同时，肠道和血液中某些氨基酸（所有生物体用于制造蛋白质的分子）的水平下降。

人体数据显示出另一个有趣的模式。研究中的老年成年人肠道微生物组中菊粉梭菌的水平往往低于年轻参与者。这与年龄增长普遍出现的肌肉力量下降的普遍模式相符。

在人类身上，目前尚不清楚肠道细菌是否影响肌肉力量，还是说更强壮、更活跃的人肠道中只是有不同的微生物。但小鼠实验暗示这种微生物可以直接增强肌肉力量，因此需要更大规模的人体研究来确定这种关系的方向。

肌肉微生物

这项研究提出的一个可能性是益生菌的未来应用。这些产品含有旨在有益健康的活微生物。如果进一步研究证实菊粉梭菌在人体中支持肌肉力量，那么它可能被开发成一种益生菌，旨在帮助人们随着年龄增长维持肌肉功能。

然而，补充剂并非鼓励肠道有益微生物的唯一方式。饮食在塑造微生物组方面发挥着重要作用。

益生元纤维作为肠道细菌的食物，也能支持它们的生长。这是因为为这些微生物提供食物能让它们在肠道中更稳定和活跃。

“菊粉梭菌”这个名字暗示了这种细菌偏爱的食物来源。它指的是菊粉，一种天然存在于洋葱、大蒜、韭菜、芦笋和菊苣根等食物中的膳食纤维。这些纤维已知能支持其他有益肠道细菌的生长，包括罗斯氏菌属（Roseburia group）的成员。

高纤维饮食长期以来与多种健康益处相关。大量研究已将较高的纤维摄入量与较低的心脏病、2型糖尿病和某些癌症风险联系起来。这些效应可能由许多不同微生物的复杂活动所驱动，而非单一物种。因此，目前，补充任何单一细菌都不能替代高纤维饮食。

然而，该研究确实有一些需要注意的局限性。人类研究组规模相对较小，且证明因果关系的实验是在小鼠而非人类身上进行的。研究中包含的老年成年人也均为男性。即便如此，这些发现进一步证明了肠道微生物组可能影响比之前认为的更多健康方面。

目前，支持肌肉力量和健康微生物组的建议仍然是熟悉的且令人放心的：定期的力量训练和富含纤维的饮食。
