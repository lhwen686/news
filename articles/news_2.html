Researchers call for governing health data as a public utility to unlock real-world evidence and protect patients

Electronic health records (EHRs); insurance claims; patient registries; wearable tech: Never before has health data been so abundant. However, the United States continues to struggle with siloed health data systems that make it difficult for critical data to be streamlined and used for the advancement of science and medicine—particularly public health, safety monitoring, and precision medicine.

A multidisciplinary team of researchers, clinicians, technologists, and policy experts, led by the UNC School of Medicine, has published a paper in Science calling for real-world health data (RWD) to be governed as a public utility, an essential public infrastructure akin to electricity, water, and the internet.

"Health data are now as fundamental to society as power grids and clean water," said Melissa Haendel, Ph.D., the Sarah Graham Kenan Distinguished Professor of genetics and first author on the paper. "Yet unlike other essential utilities, health data infrastructure is largely voluntary, fragmented, and economically misaligned. We need a model that treats data as shared infrastructure, governed for the public good, while protecting patient autonomy and trust."

To address data silos, patient privacy, and other related challenges, researchers looked to lessons learned from the creation of public utilities throughout history, such as the electrification movement, federal drinking water standards, and the advent of the internet.

They propose that a public utility model could help better govern health data and get data to where it matters: from the people to new and innovative solutions and back.

"We take for granted having ready access to high-quality clinical data, but that access really depends on the existence of consistently funded, reliable, and secure data infrastructure," said co-author Emily Pfaff, Ph.D., who is an associate professor of medicine at the UNC School of Medicine.

"On the research side, that consistent funding is not guaranteed, leading to eventual degradation or disappearance of important data repositories. To prevent this, we can learn from the public utility model, which comes with a guarantee of that long-term infrastructure investment."

The public utility approach would ensure that there is governmental regulatory oversight, ensuring fair access, transparency, and accountability for health data.

Governance would be distributed to utility boards, while the government would establish and enforce regulations and standards, enabling individual research entities and stakeholders the freedom to address their own research questions and challenges. Like other public utility boards, this information could be accessed by patients and the community, ultimately empowering with a formal role in oversight and data sharing.

The researchers also argue that current U.S. privacy laws, including HIPAA, are insufficient for today's digital health ecosystem. They call for extending HIPAA-like protections to include consumer health data (including wearables, genomics, and mobile apps), clarifying public health and research use cases, and more.

"HIPAA was written before mobile health apps, genomic sequencing, and AI-driven analytics," said Haendel. "We must modernize privacy protections while ensuring that patients, not just institutions, benefit from the evidence their data generate."

Haendel and the researchers also recognized that transforming health data governance will require both legal reform and cultural change. They underscore that health care organizations and policymakers must recognize health data as shared infrastructure with collective value, not just a secondary byproduct of clinical care.

"To fully realize precision medicine, drug safety, and population health for everyone, we must treat real-world data as an essential resource," Haendel said. "A government-regulated, community-owned, federated model can balance innovation with trust, without resorting to centralized data ownership."

电子健康记录（EHRs）；保险理赔；患者登记系统；可穿戴技术：健康数据从未如此丰富。然而，美国仍在与孤立的健康数据系统作斗争，这些系统使得关键数据难以实现流线化，并用于推动科学和医学的进步——特别是公共卫生、安全监测和精准医疗领域。

由北卡罗来纳大学医学院（UNC School of Medicine）领导的一个由研究人员、临床医生、技术专家和政策专家组成的多学科团队，在《科学》杂志上发表了一篇论文，呼吁将真实世界健康数据（RWD）作为公共事业进行管理，将其视为类似于电力、水和互联网等基本公共基础设施。

“健康数据现在对社会的重要性堪比电网和清洁水。”该论文的第一作者、Sarah Graham Kenan遗传学杰出教授Melissa Haendel博士说道。“然而，与其他基本公共事业不同，健康数据基础设施在很大程度上是自愿的、碎片化的，并且经济上存在错位。我们需要一种将数据视为共享基础设施、为公共利益进行管理、同时保护患者自主权和信任的模型。”

为了解决数据孤岛、患者隐私及其他相关挑战，研究人员借鉴了历史上公共事业创建过程中吸取的经验教训，例如电气化运动、联邦饮用水标准以及互联网的出现。

他们提出，公共事业模式有助于更好地管理健康数据，并将数据传送到它应该去的地方：从人民手中到创新解决方案，再反馈给人民。

“我们想当然地认为可以随时获取高质量的临床数据，但这种获取实际上依赖于持续获得资金支持、可靠且安全的数据基础设施的存在。”北卡罗来纳大学医学院医学副教授、共同作者Emily Pfaff博士说道。

“在研究方面，这种持续的资金支持没有保障，导致重要数据存储库最终退化或消失。为了防止这种情况，我们可以借鉴公共事业模式，它能保证对基础设施进行长期投资。”

公共事业方法将确保政府的监管，保障健康数据的公平获取、透明度和问责制。

管理权将分配给公共事业委员会，而政府将制定并执行法规和标准，使独立的科研实体和利益相关者能够自由地解决自己的研究问题和挑战。像其他公共事业委员会一样，患者和社区也可以访问这些信息，最终通过正式的监督和数据共享角色获得赋能。

研究人员还指出，包括HIPAA在内的当前美国隐私法对于当今的数字健康生态系统而言是不足的。他们呼吁将类似HIPAA的保护范围扩展到包括消费者健康数据（包括可穿戴设备、基因组学和移动应用程序），明确公共卫生和研究的使用案例等。

“HIPAA是在移动健康应用程序、基因组测序和人工智能驱动分析出现之前制定的。”Haendel说道。“我们必须现代化隐私保护措施，同时确保患者，而不仅仅是机构，能从他们数据产生的证据中受益。”

Haendel和研究人员还认识到，改变健康数据治理将需要法律改革和文化变革。他们强调，医疗机构和政策制定者必须将健康数据视为具有集体价值的共享基础设施，而不仅仅是临床护理的次要副产品。

“为了充分实现精准医疗、药物安全和全民健康，我们必须将真实世界数据视为一项基本资源。”Haendel说道。“一个由政府监管、社区所有、联邦化的模式可以在创新与信任之间取得平衡，而无需诉诸于集中的数据所有权。”
