Original Text
Although mental health responses after disaster often begin with symptom checklists, triage, and specialist treatment, evidence from Japan shows that this approach is necessary but not sufficient. After the Great East Japan Earthquake in 2011, disaster-related deaths were time-dependent, with nearly half occurring within 1 month and 78% within 3 months.1 However, mental health needs often persist much longer in affected populations. Although acute mental health assessment matters, long-term recovery also depends on whether people can continue to live safely, sustain relationships, and recover social roles after displacement.
