脑部扫描揭示为何即使饱腹仍难以抵抗零食

Brain scans reveal why you can't resist a snack, even when you're full

Research from the University of East Anglia (UEA) may finally explain why we still reach for the cookie jar, even when we're full. A new study reveals that the human brain continues to respond to tempting food cues even after we've eaten enough. The findings are published in the journal Appetite.

In a world of endless advertisements and snack cues on every corner, the team says their findings shed light on why so many of us struggle to maintain a healthy weight.

Lead researcher Dr. Thomas Sambrook, from UEA's School of Psychology, said, "Obesity has become a major worldwide health crisis. But rising obesity isn't simply about willpower—it's a sign that our food-rich environments and learned responses to mouth-watering cues are overpowering the body's natural appetite controls.

"We wanted to better understand how our brains react to food cues when we are already feeling full. We studied people's brainwaves after eating and found that even though their stomachs might be satisfied, their brains didn't seem to care. In fact, no amount of fullness could switch off the brain's response to delicious-looking food. This suggests that food cues may trigger overeating in the absence of hunger."

How the research happened

In the study, 76 volunteers were monitored using electroencephalogram (EEG) brain scans as they played a reward‑based learning game with food such as sweets, chocolate, crisps and popcorn. Halfway through the task, participants were given a meal of one of the foods until they didn't want another bite.

According to the researchers, the participants really were full—they reported dramatically reduced desire for the food, and their behavior showed they no longer valued it. But their brains told a different story.

Electrical activity in areas associated with reward continued responding just as strongly to images of the now‑unwanted food even after participants were completely full.

Dr. Sambrook said, "What we saw is that the brain simply refuses to downgrade how rewarding a food looks, no matter how full you are. Even when people know they don't want the food, even when their behavior shows they've stopped valuing the food—their brains continue to fire 'reward!' signals the moment the food appears. It's a recipe for overeating."

A habit you didn't know you had

The findings suggest that our responses to food cues may work like habits—automatic, learned reactions forged over years of pairing certain foods with pleasure.

Dr. Sambrook said, "These habitual brain responses may operate independently of our conscious decisions. So, while you might think you're eating because you're hungry, your brain may simply be following a well‑worn script."

The study found no link between people's ability to make goal‑directed decisions and their brain's resistance to food devaluation. That means even people with excellent self‑control can be undermined by automatic neural responses.

"If you're struggling with late‑night snacking or can't say no to treats even when you're full, the problem may not be your discipline—it may be your brain's built‑in wiring," said Dr. Sambrook. "It's really no wonder that resisting a doughnut can feel impossible."

This research was led by UEA in collaboration with the University of Plymouth.

来自东英吉利大学（UEA）的研究或许最终能解释为什么即使我们已经饱足，仍然会伸手去拿饼干。一项新研究揭示，即使在我们摄入充足食物后，人类大脑仍会继续对诱人的食物线索做出反应。该研究结果已发表在《食欲》（Appetite）杂志上。

研究团队表示，在一个充斥着无休止的广告和随处可见的零食线索的世界里，他们的发现阐明了为什么我们如此多的人难以维持健康的体重。

东英吉利大学心理学学院的主要研究员托马斯·桑布鲁克博士说：“肥胖已成为全球性的主要健康危机。但肥胖率上升并非仅仅关乎意志力——这表明我们食物丰富的环境以及对令人垂涎的线索形成的习得性反应，正在压倒身体的自然食欲控制机制。”

“我们希望更好地了解当我们已经感到饱足时，大脑如何对食物线索做出反应。我们研究了人们进食后的脑电波，发现即使他们的胃部可能已经满足，他们的大脑似乎并不在意。事实上，无论饱足感多强，都无法抑制大脑对诱人食物的反应。这表明食物线索可能在没有饥饿感的情况下触发过度进食。”

研究过程

在这项研究中，76名志愿者在参与一项基于奖励的学习游戏时，通过脑电图（EEG）进行监测，游戏中涉及糖果、巧克力、薯片和爆米花等食物。任务进行到一半时，参与者被安排食用其中一种食物，直到他们不再想吃一口为止。

研究人员表示，参与者确实感到饱足——他们报告称对食物的渴望显著降低，并且他们的行为也表明他们不再看重这种食物。但他们的大脑却呈现出不同的情况。

即使在参与者完全饱足之后，与奖励相关的区域的电活动仍对这些此时已不被渴望的食物图像做出同样强烈的反应。

桑布鲁克博士说：“我们看到的是，无论你有多饱，大脑都拒绝降低食物看起来的奖励吸引力。即使人们知道他们不想要这种食物，即使他们的行为表明他们已不再看重这种食物——大脑在食物出现的那一刻仍然会持续发出‘奖励！’信号。这为过度进食埋下了伏笔。”

一个你未曾察觉的习惯

研究结果表明，我们对食物线索的反应可能类似于习惯——是经年累月将特定食物与愉悦感配对而形成的自动、习得性反应。

桑布鲁克博士说：“这些习惯性脑部反应可能独立于我们的意识决策而运作。因此，虽然你可能认为自己进食是因为饥饿，但你的大脑可能只是遵循一个根深蒂固的模式。”

研究发现，人们进行目标导向决策的能力与大脑对食物价值降低的抵抗之间没有关联。这意味着即使是那些拥有极佳自我控制能力的人，也可能受到自动神经反应的影响。

桑布鲁克博士说：“如果你正在为深夜零食而挣扎，或者即使饱腹也无法拒绝美食，问题可能不在于你的自制力——而在于你大脑的内在连接机制。难怪抵抗一个甜甜圈会感觉不可能。”

这项研究由东英吉利大学主导，并与普利茅斯大学合作完成。
