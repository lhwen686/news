Original Text
Prostate cancer affects one in five Australian men, making it the most common cancer in the country. Now, researchers at the Garvan Institute of Medical Research have produced the world's most detailed cellular 'atlas' of early-stage prostate cancer, revealing the earliest changes that lead to the disease.

The study describes a never-before-identified cell type and shows that many cells appearing healthy under the microscope have already begun their journey towards malignancy. These findings could reveal new risk factors, lead to earlier detection methods and help determine which patients are at risk of developing aggressive disease. The study was published in Cancer Research, a journal of the American Association for Cancer Research.

The research team, led by Professor Alexander Swarbrick, Co-Director of the Cancer Ecosystems Program and Lab Head at Garvan, analyzed tissue from 24 patients recently diagnosed with early-stage prostate cancer. The samples were drawn from the Garvan St Vincent's Prostate Cancer Biobank – the largest in the southern hemisphere, with contributions from more than 16,000 patients over three decades. The team then used advanced single-cell RNA sequencing to examine the genetic activity of every single cell in a tissue, and spatial transcriptomics to map their exact locations in the tumor landscape.

This resulted in a comprehensive atlas, identifying 11 major cell types, 50 minor subtypes, and numerous cell state classifications within prostate cancer tissue. This level of detail provides the global research community with an invaluable resource for studying how the tumors form and opportunities to design new therapeutics.

We've captured a molecular readout showing years of cancer development. What's particularly surprising is the significant number of cells that look perfectly ordinary but have already acquired DNA changes associated with cancer. This suggests that current diagnostic methods using conventional microscopy may not be granular enough to detect these early mutations." Professor Alexander Swarbrick, Co-Director of the Cancer Ecosystems Program and Lab Head at Garvan

Implications for early detection

Standard diagnosis relies on visually identifying structural irregularities in cells under a microscope. However, this study revealed that the progression from healthy prostate tissue to cancer involves multiple cellular states that are invisible. The researchers found that many epithelial cells – the cells that line organs and are where most cancers originate – showed early genomic alterations, even in tissue that appeared 'normal'. This suggests that molecular testing could one day be used to detect these invisible warning signs, potentially allowing for diagnosis before the cancer can be seen by a pathologist.

Clinical lead Professor Anthony Joshua, Lab Head at Garvan and Head of the Department of Medical Oncology at St Vincent's Hospital Sydney, emphasises the clinical significance of these findings. "Currently we don't know how prostate cancer starts. Understanding the sequence of events that lead to life-threatening disease – and finding the signatures of cancer in normal-looking cells – is essential for creating better predictive tests and therapies.

"While most prostate cancer patients respond well to current treatments, this atlas provides foundational knowledge of the disease's first stages that could help us pinpoint those at high risk of dangerous progression, who need more intensive intervention," he says.

Surprise discovery of new cell type

Among the study's most significant findings is the identification of a previously unknown cell type – called perineural cancer-associated fibroblasts, a type of connective tissue cell located near nerves in cancers.

"These perineural fibroblasts in tumors appear to have specialised machinery to communicate with nerves. We don't yet know what these cells are doing, but the role of nerves in tumors is getting increasing attention in the cancer field. We've been able to classify these cells at the molecular level for the first time, giving them a distinct identity," says Professor Swarbrick.

The tendency for tumors to grow in and around nerves in prostate cancer has long been associated with worse patient outcomes. This discovery of specialised cells that interact with these nerves provides a new piece of the puzzle in understanding how prostate tumors may recruit nerves, or spread to other parts of the body along the nerves to cause metastatic disease.

Next steps

The research team plans to expand the number of patients studied and investigate specific cellular populations, particularly those cells that look healthy but harbour genetic changes.

"We've had a peek through the window, but it's still somewhat blurry," says Professor Swarbrick. "Now we have more precise questions about these pre-malignant cells, and we can apply more focused techniques to be crystal clear about the sequence of genetic events that drive this cancer."

Future work will also focus on understanding the function of the newly discovered perineural fibroblasts, which may reveal why prostate tumors that contain more nerves have worse outcomes.

While the team emphasizes this is fundamental science rather than of immediate clinical application, the findings pave the way for future development of predictive tests. Given the high incidence of prostate cancer, even small improvements in early detection and treatment selection could benefit thousands of Australians.

前列腺癌影响着五分之一的澳大利亚男性，使其成为该国最常见的癌症。现在，加尔文医学研究所的研究人员已经绘制出世界上最详细的早期前列腺癌细胞“图谱”，揭示了导致该疾病的最早期变化。

这项研究描述了一种前所未见的细胞类型，并表明许多在显微镜下看似健康的细胞已经开始向恶性转化。这些发现可能揭示新的风险因素，促成更早的检测方法，并有助于确定哪些患者有发展为侵袭性疾病的风险。该研究发表在美国癌症研究协会的期刊《癌症研究》上。

该研究团队由加尔文研究所癌症生态系统项目联合主任兼实验室主任亚历山大·斯沃布里克教授领导，他们分析了24名近期被诊断为早期前列腺癌患者的组织。样本来源于加尔文圣文森特前列腺癌生物样本库——这是南半球最大的生物样本库，在过去三十年中汇集了超过16,000名患者的贡献。随后，该团队利用先进的单细胞RNA测序技术检测了组织中每个细胞的基因活动，并利用空间转录组学技术绘制了它们在肿瘤微环境中的精确位置。

这最终形成了一份全面的图谱，识别出前列腺癌组织中的11种主要细胞类型、50种次要亚型以及众多细胞状态分类。这种详细程度为全球研究界提供了一份宝贵的资源，用于研究肿瘤的形成机制以及设计新疗法的机会。

“我们捕获了显示多年癌症发展的分子读数。特别令人惊讶的是，大量看似完全正常的细胞却已经获得了与癌症相关的DNA变化。这表明，目前使用传统显微镜的诊断方法可能不够精细，无法检测到这些早期突变。”加尔文研究所癌症生态系统项目联合主任兼实验室主任亚历山大·斯沃布里克教授说道。

对早期检测的启示

标准诊断依赖于在显微镜下目视识别细胞的结构异常。然而，这项研究揭示，从健康前列腺组织到癌症的进展涉及多种肉眼不可见的细胞状态。研究人员发现，许多上皮细胞——即构成器官内壁且大多数癌症起源于此的细胞——即使在看似“正常”的组织中也显示出早期基因组改变。这表明，分子检测有一天可能被用于检测这些隐形的预警信号，从而有可能在病理学家能够观察到癌症之前进行诊断。

加尔文研究所实验室主任兼悉尼圣文森特医院医学肿瘤科主任、临床负责人安东尼·约书亚教授强调了这些发现的临床意义。“目前我们尚不清楚前列腺癌是如何开始的。了解导致危及生命的疾病的事件序列，并在看似正常的细胞中发现癌症的‘印记’，对于创建更好的预测性检测和治疗至关重要。”

他表示：“尽管大多数前列腺癌患者对目前的治疗反应良好，但这份图谱为疾病的早期阶段提供了基础知识，有助于我们精确识别那些具有高危进展风险、需要更密集干预的患者。”

新细胞类型的惊人发现

这项研究最重大的发现之一是识别出了一种以前未知的细胞类型——名为“癌周神经相关成纤维细胞”，这是一种位于癌症神经附近的结缔组织细胞。

斯沃布里克教授说：“肿瘤中的这些癌周神经相关成纤维细胞似乎拥有专门的机制与神经进行沟通。我们尚不清楚这些细胞的具体功能，但神经在肿瘤中的作用在癌症研究领域正日益受到关注。我们首次在分子层面分类了这些细胞，赋予了它们独特的身份。”

前列腺癌中肿瘤在神经内及神经周围生长的趋势，长期以来一直与较差的患者预后相关。此次发现的与神经相互作用的特化细胞，为理解前列腺肿瘤如何募集神经，或沿神经扩散到身体其他部位从而导致转移性疾病提供了新的线索。

下一步计划

研究团队计划扩大研究患者数量，并深入调查特定的细胞群，特别是那些看似健康但却携带基因改变的细胞。

斯沃布里克教授说：“我们已经通过窗户窥视了一番，但画面仍有些模糊。现在我们对这些癌前细胞有了更精确的问题，我们可以应用更集中的技术来清晰地揭示驱动这种癌症的基因事件序列。”

未来的工作还将专注于理解新发现的癌周神经相关成纤维细胞的功能，这可能揭示为何含有更多神经的前列腺肿瘤预后较差。

尽管团队强调这是一项基础科学研究，并非立即具有临床应用价值，但这些发现为未来预测性检测的开发铺平了道路。鉴于前列腺癌的高发病率，即使早期检测和治疗选择方面取得微小改进，也可能使成千上万的澳大利亚人受益。
